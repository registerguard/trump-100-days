100 days

4th Congressional District residents on President Trump

By Andy Nelson | The Register-Guard
April 29, 2017

The first 100 days of a new term has been seen as an early benchmark for presidents since 1933 when Franklin D. Roosevelt passed a flurry of bills in that time. During the 2016 campaign, then-candidate Donald Trump issued a “100-day action plan to Make America Great Again” contract for his supporters.

Conversations with a dozen voters from Oregon’s Fourth Congressional District reveal a range of emotions and perspectives on Trump’s first 100 days. More conservative voters remain solidly behind Trump despite early stumbles, while those who did not support him remain energized as they develop long-term strategies for action.

An analysis of the 2016 presidential election by Daily Kos found that this district was the most closely divided in the United States. Hillary Clinton prevailed over Trump, 46.1 percent to 46.0 percent — a mere 554 votes.

The divisions remain deep. Linda Lynch, president of the Lane County League of Women Voters, noted that civility is often in short supply. As the country moves past the 100-day mark, the question of whether those divisions begin to close, or continue to widen, is open for debate.

Linda Lynch

Eugene, age 71 – Lynch is president of Lane County Chapter of the League of Women Voters.

It’s become a popular campaign effort to say, ‘In my first 100 days, I will … .’ And then confronted by the realities of office and checks and balances in government, you find it is pretty hard to get a lot in 100 days, which is probably a good thing.”

Alan Zundel

Eugene, age 64 – Zundel is the co-chair of the Pacific Green Party of Oregon. He ran for Oregon Secretary of State in 2016.

I think his overriding objective is to get respect for himself. That seems to be his motivator ... . I don’t see a real political program there because he changes all the time. And he takes pride in that, as though it’s a virtue to not to have any guiding philosophy or consistency. I don’t think that works too well when you are the leader of a major nation.”

Ellie Keeland

Reedsport, age 69 – Keeland is a chainsaw carver and owner of The Log Lot.

He likes guns, he likes money, he likes private enterprise — the things we were built on. A good American. He’s American. A true American. He didn’t do it for the wage, that’s for sure. He didn’t need the power — he’s got plenty. He loves America.”

Jan Dial

Springfield – Dial was a door gunner during the Vietnam War.

I think the people who voted for him because they were disillusioned and discouraged are going to be even more so in 200, 300 days from now. I think we’ll be in a war again. I don’t think the man has the emotional stability, the intelligence, the morals to be in that place. The ethics. I don’t have high expectations of his having a productive and successful administration for our nation.”

Don Crouse

Springfield, age 65 – Crouse is a retired truck driver and Vietnam War veteran.

Personally, I think he’s doing a helluva fine job. He’s keeping up with his promises. The biggest problem he has is people stabbing him in the back, his own party considered. We hired them to work for us, not for themselves. I don’t care how many of them want to make themselves millionaires. Take care of people in the United States. Americans.

“As far as Trump and his promises, he’s doing his best. He’s just getting stabbed in the back way too much. I’ve got his back.”

Robin Meacher

Eugene, age 30 – Meacher is a lawyer working on issues with local environmental organizations.

I think that working locally, not just in Eugene, but within our states and with our communities, is where we are going to be able to really combat whatever this willy-nilly administration continues to do.”

Dallas Petenbrink

North Bend, age 41 – Petenbrink is a Coos County Republican Precinct Committee Person. He moved to Oregon from Louisiana in 1992.

I don’t think anybody is ever prepared to be president of the United States. I don’t think you can be. The weight of the office has got to be overwhelming. And the learning curve is huge.”

Ericka Thessen

Eugene, age 43 – Thessen is a member of Indivisible Eugene and NOW. She was a regular voter but not politically active until Trump was elected.

A lot of us are looking at it as blessing and an opportunity that we can be better. We have to do better as a nation for us to be what we want to be. The great American story is that we are going to take this thing and we’ll make something amazing out of it. We are going to pull ourselves up by the bootstraps — isn’t that kind of the American way ... ?

“We’ve got to be able to address those ugly parts that I think we do have. There is inequity — financially and racially ... . We can’t fix it until we acknowledge that it’s there. So, I think it’s a blessing ... . It needed to happen ... . And I feel like things are going to be better but it really helped to show this divide is massive.”

Rodney Schilling

Hauser – Schilling is the chairman of the Coos County Republican Central Committee.

He’s a businessman. I was a businessman. And I think unraveling the laws and rules and many government agencies that are holding business down and keeping our GDP at the lowest it’s been in years and years is going to be one of the best things he can do.”

Silver Mogart

Eugene, age 42 – Mogart is concerned community member, father and comic.

He’s in. We live with that. There’s things that need to happen, there are people that are hurting and the rhetoric he has been putting out there has had a tremendous effect on a huge population of the United States. That should wake him up. That should bring him to a place where he’s thinking about the rest of America, not just those that voted for him. That’s what a good president would do.

Teri Grier

North Bend, age 52 – Grier is District 4 director of the Federation of Republican Women of Oregon. She ran for Oregon House of Representatives District 9, losing to Democrat Caddy McKeown.

In rural Oregon, they really showed up for our president. They are engaged and continuing to be engaged. ... And that’s something that was a little bit surprising because people are usually a little burned out and they go home and rest for another four years and then come back out.”

“I heard (Trump) at almost every single event, ‘We are going to build a wall and we are going to make Mexico pay for it.’ Now that he is sitting in the office, he is realizing the importance of diplomacy. ... You have to adjust if you want to be successful... .”

Amanda Rudd

Coquille, age 26 – Rudd grew up in Coquille and has returned to the community as a substitute teacher in area schools. She’s also on the City of Coquille Budget Committee.

As somebody who is a teacher, and seeing the appointments that are being made with (new U.S. Education Secretary Betsy) DeVos, it’s concerning for myself and probably many others because I’m a huge advocate for public education, and she’s not. So it’s hard to say what’s going to happen.”
